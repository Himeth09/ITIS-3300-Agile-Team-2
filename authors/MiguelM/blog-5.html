What is Project Quality Management?

5 March, 2022

Definition and Context

Project Quality Management is often regarded as a rather complex topic within the context of Project Management. However, for the sake of having a point of reference, an author defines Project Quality Management as “the planning, scoping, implementing, and monitoring of quality into all phases of the project from concept through the delivery aspects of the work” (Patterson, 1983). While the context may indicate that "quality" denotes "perfection," it is typically more about establishing consistency in quality throughout a project in this example. However, what is meant by "quality" depends on what the client or stakeholder expects from the project, and so varies from project to project. Quality management and project management are complimentary in today's world. They both place a premium on client happiness and the assumption that quality leads to customer contentment. The primary goal of project quality management is to ensure that the project fits the needs for which it was designed—nothing more, nothing less. To put it another way, you must fulfill the demands of the stakeholder to assure quality. Project quality management, on the other hand, does not include meeting or surpassing standards. It is the project manager's and project management team's obligation to strike a balance between quality and grade (a category or rank assigned to products or services having the same functional use but different technical characteristics).This duty guarantees that quality standards are met. This suggests that while having a low-quality, high-quality product is conceivable and justifiable, having a low-quality product is never acceptable. The requirements are established with the stakeholders at the start of the project. These specifications serve as the foundation for the project's work. After then, the project manager's role is to make sure that everything runs well. Giving the consumer additional or doing extra work is not what quality is about. The concept of extras is frequently founded on erroneous assumptions about what the buyer wants. These add time, money, and other disadvantages to a project, but they don't necessarily result in higher client satisfaction.

Conformance to Requirements

Another important concept to understand in order to gain a better understanding of Project Quality Management is conformance to requirements. An expert in the field defines conformance requirements as “are the expression, in the form of a statement, which conveys the criteria to be fulfilled” (Rosenthal, 2001). A project management plan's quality assurance relies heavily on conformance. Without it, the project's overall performance will suffer. Conformance, by definition, is a notion that produces a result that is within the quality requirement's acceptable range. This indicates that conformance refers to a product's, service's, or process' capacity to fulfill the buyer's and customer's intended standards. Conformance is viewed as something that is acceptable because the specification is a representation of what the buyers need. Conformance is normally measured within an allowable tolerance range, therefore a product that falls inside that range has a good conformance quality. Conformance is a project management strategy for tracking a product's or service's long-term viability. The product or service is evaluated near the performance barrier that has been specified. The measurement is in compliance with the standards if it stays within the bounds. If this is not the case, management focuses on the product or service and corrects the problem. It is difficult for project managers to establish the acceptance of their product, service, or process without compliance.

Fitness For Use

A very important idea in Project Quality Management involves the idea of how apt a product or service is for use. Speaking on the subject, an expert defines fitness of use as “taking customers' requirements and expectations into account, which involves whether the products or services fit their uses”(Stefanska). Because various consumers may utilize things in different ways, products must include diverse characteristics of fitness in order to be used. Each of these characteristics is a quality feature, and they may all be categorized into metrics for suitability for use. The suitability of information is determined by comparing its quality elements to a set of standards that indicate the equivalent acceptable quality components. The standards are established using the same components that are used to describe data quality in order to simplify direct comparison. The allowed margin of uncertainty in the final decision ultimately determines information standards. The benefit of fitness for use is that it allows for less stringent standards in most circumstances. This is far more efficient in terms of the time periods and expenses associated with Risk Management tasks. Furthermore, many scientists, consultants, and regulators, as well as the general public. The idea of fitness for usage is rational. This notion is based on the premise that, like most things in life, objects must be designed for a specific, proper function. A garage, for example, must be adequate for storing a car rather than serving as a children's playground.

Processes of Quality Management

Project Quality Management can be easily broken down into three main processes in terms of quality: planning, assurance, and control. The formulation of quality measurements is one of the most crucial components of quality planning. Beyond the usual measures of scope, time, and money, project managers must think beyond the box. It is critical to connect a project to the company's, organization's, or business unit's strategic goals. According to the article Project Management & Six Sigma, Use Six Sigma Methods for Better Project Results (Rever, 2006), a project manager's efforts on each project he or she is overseeing should result in some sort of business improvement. This is, after all, why a project exists: to improve some aspect of the business. Measuring progress is necessary to demonstrate progress. It is critical for a project manager to have active attention, engagement, and support from important sponsor. The quality assurance process is linked to process analysis and continuous improvement. It is critical to have precise data before quality standards can be validated; as the old adage goes, "garbage in, trash out." As a result, every project team should undertake a complete measurement system analysis to ensure that the measuring system and data are accurate and reliable. Quality control is the final step in the project quality management process. Quality control is keeping track of the project metrics specified during the quality planning phase to verify they are operating as expected. Understanding the notion of variance as well as how to properly communicate with data are all part of quality control. Measures were defined during the quality planning stage, and quality assurance included acquiring reliable data for those metrics. In the quality control process, graphical analysis tools are used to present data so that choices about the quality of the process's output may be made quickly and simply. With this phase, it's easy to get into "analytical paralysis," so staying on course is crucial. All in all, Project Quality Management is essential to good and effective Project Management skills.
