Strategic Planning and Project Selection

5 February, 2022

Someone once said, “he who heads anywhere, arrives nowhere”. In other words, setting out to do something without a clear idea of the goal in mind ultimately amounts to nothing. No progress can be made when there is an unclear definition of what the chief end is. Planning is paramount. The Merriam-Webster Dictionary defines planning as “the act or process of making a plan to achieve or do something” (Merriam-Webster). In the context of Project Management and Agile, specifically, the responsibility to devise a plan and shine a light on the road ahead lies with the leader: the Scrum Master. These people are hold the authority to make decisions regarding the team and the project itself and are also expected to shoulder the responsibility of seeing the process through to the end. Furthermore, Scrum Masters are also expected to develop a plan with the organization’s specific objectives and mission in mind. Naturally, in order to make this plan a success, a strategy is needed. In the context of Project Integration Management, Strategic Planning and Project Selection both play an essential role. According to the Balanced Scorecard Institute, Strategic Planning is “an organizational management activity that is used to set priorities, focus energy and resources, strengthen operations, ensure that employees and other stakeholders are working toward common goals, establish agreement around intended outcomes/results, and assess and adjust the organization’s direction in response to a changing environment” (Balanced Scorecard Institute). In other words, planning precedes results. One of the most prominent tools for effective strategic planning is using the SWOT analysis. This tool is “a framework used to evaluate a company's competitive position and to develop strategic planning” (Kenton, 2022). SWOT incorporates four different quadrants as part of the analysis process. The four quadrants are: Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Strengths are what an organization is adept at doing and what sets it apart from its direct competition (Kenton, 2022). Most of the time, “playing to one’s strengths”, as the saying goes, is the best decision when in a competitive environment. In the case of most organizations where profit is the end goal, doing that which they know provides the most value to the customer is almost always the right way to go. Weaknesses refer to that which impedes a company from performing at the highest level it can, and that which it needs to improve in order to stay ahead of its competitors (Kenton, 2022). The saying “it’s easier to catch up than to stay ahead” carries much weight when it comes to this topic. What most organizations at the top struggle with at times is not playing to their strengths but minimizing their weaknesses, and these weaknesses create exploitable pockets which competing organizations will do their utmost to capitalize on in order to seize an advantage. Opportunities are “favorable external factors that could give an organization a competitive advantage” (Kenton, 2022). Creating opportunities is often a very difficult endeavor to undertake in most aspects of life, as opportunities often are things outside of an organization’s control. However, what an organization always has control of is its own actions and must, therefore, strive to seize and take advantage of all opportunities that fall on its lap in order to be successful. Threats refer to “factors that have the potential to harm an organization” (Kenton, 2022). Like opportunities, threats are factors most often outside of an organization’s control and are most often hard to deal with. In most cases, a strategy needs to be formed in order to deal with threats and prevent these catastrophes waiting to happen from having a negative impact on the future of an organization. Evidently, the benefits of employing SWOT analysis to create effective strategic plans cannot be understated. The quantity and quality of the information that can be obtained from using this tool constitutes, without a doubt, one of the strongest weapons when developing a successful plan. One of the most preferred ways of involving SWOT analysis in the plan devising process is to employ what is known as a mind map. A mind map is “an easy way to brainstorm thoughts organically without worrying about order and structure” (Mind Mapping). Mind maps are usually implemented as diagrams which in represent concepts in a non-linear fashion in order to build around a topic (Mind Mapping). The end goal of using SWOT analysis and Mind Mapping to inform Strategic Planning, is to then have a clear picture of what potential projects an organization should pursue in order to achieve its predefined goals. As someone once said: “perfect practice makes perfect”. It’s not enough just to plan for the sake of planning. The planning process must be intentional and well-developed with ample input from the interested parties in order to produce a game plan that will win the game. This process should not be rushed, by any means, if an organization’s goals are to be fulfilled. Some of the most overlooked and perhaps ignored components of Project Selection is often the business aspect. Every organization, barring non-profit ones, seek to make a profit. Therefore, it stands to reason that every action an organization takes has as its main goal to provide value to its customers, in order to secure a profit. Consequently, an organization’s business strategy is unequivocally crucial to its success and growth. For this reason, it is vital that Project Managers give an organization’s business strategy the place and priority it deserves. Summarizing, strategic planning and project selection are indispensable to an organization’s future and success. The careful development of plans, informed by an organization’s chosen goals, is indubitably the key to its future growth and success. It is Scrum Masters’ duty to understand the importance of the planning process, and to ensure the whole team is made keenly aware of the plan in its entirety. Similarly, it is the Project Manager’s responsibility to also inform the plan with the organization’s business strategies and goals, in order to develop a plan that turns these into reality. When these responsibilities are upheld, the plan that follows almost certainly brings about an organization’s growth and success.
