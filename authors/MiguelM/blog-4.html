Webster's dictionary defines cost as 'something given up in exchange' (Webster). Costs are often measured in monetary amounts, such as dollars, that must be paid to acquire goods and services. Cost management is an important element in project management since it entails the procedures that must be followed to guarantee that a project team completes a project within the allocated budget. The cost of any work is the price paid by the customer to a provider in exchange for the goods or services the provider provides. The cost management processes in a business are the way in which the business accounts for costs to determine how much the company should bill the customers for all the services it provides as well as the costs it must incur to deliver the service. The cost is also known as billable cost, variable cost and indirect cost. Cost management in Project management has four components: cost planning, cost estimation, cost determination, and cost control. Each component helps to manage the cost effectively.

First off, cost management planning includes identifying the rules, methods, and documentation that will be utilized for project cost planning, execution, and control. A cost management strategy is the key product of this approach. Secondly, developing an approximation or estimate of the expenses of the resources required to accomplish a project is known as cost estimation. Activity cost estimates, foundation of estimates, and project document updates are the key outputs of the cost estimating process. Thirdly, allocating the total cost estimate to individual work items to provide a baseline for monitoring performance is part of determining the budget. A cost baseline, project financing requirements, and project document changes are the major outcomes of the cost budgeting process. Lastly, controlling expenses entails keeping tabs on the project's budget. Work performance information, cost predictions, change requests, project management plan updates, and project document updates are the major outputs of the cost control process.

Many IT initiatives never get started because IT experts don't know how to make a business case for them. Important ideas to master for a better understanding of project cost management include net present value analysis, return on investment, and payback analysis. Also, a basic understanding of the underlying principles of economics can go a long way in developing a more complete picture of the costs involved in a project. Revenues minus outgoings equals profits. A corporation can boost profits by increasing sales, cutting costs, or combining the two. Profits are more important to most CEOs than other considerations. When it comes to justifying new information system expenditures, there are a few things to keep in mind. It's critical to concentrate on the influence on profitability, rather than merely revenues or costs, when it comes to innovation and technology. Without knowing the profit margin, any program in question would be useless. The profit margin is the percentage of revenue that is made up of profits. A profit margin of 2% means that $100 in revenue generates $2 in profit. There is a 22 percent profit margin if the corporation loses $2 for every $100 in revenue.

Another important concept to know is what life cycle costing is in the context of Project Management. A big-picture view of a project's cost throughout its life cycle is provided by life cycle costing. This aids in the development of an accurate financial cost and benefit prediction for a project. The entire cost of ownership, or development plus support expenses, for a project is considered in life cycle costing. The approach of estimating the projected yearly expenses and benefits for a project, as well as the resulting annual cash flow, is known as cash flow analysis. To calculate net present value, project managers must undertake a cash flow analysis. Most people grasp the fundamental notion of cash flow: they can't buy something if they don't have enough money in their wallets or bank accounts. When deciding which initiatives to invest in, top management must consider cash flow considerations.

Costs and benefits can be classified as tangible or intangible, depending on how effectively a company can identify the expected costs and benefits for a project. In dollars, tangible costs or advantages are straightforward to calculate. Intangible costs and benefits, on the other hand, are difficult to quantify in terms of money. Direct costs are those that are directly tied to the creation of the project's products and services. Direct expenses might be assigned to a specific project. Direct expenses, for example, include the pay of full-time project employees as well as the cost of gear and software acquired particularly for the project. Direct costs can be managed, thus project managers should concentrate on these.

Indirect expenses are costs that are not directly connected to the project's goods or services, but are indirectly tied to the project's completion. In a huge facility with 1,000 people working on many projects, indirect expenses may include the cost of energy, paper towels, and other essentials. Project managers have relatively limited influence over indirect expenditures, which are given to projects. Money that has already been spent is referred to as sunk cost. As if it were a sinking ship that could never be resurrected. Sunk expenses should not be considered when determining which initiatives to invest in or continue. According to the learning curve principle, when more units are produced, the unit cost of those products drops in a predictable fashion. Contingency reserves are included in the project cost baseline to account for future scenarios that can only be partially anticipated (also known as known unknowns). Unpredictable future situations are accommodated by management reserves (sometimes called unknown unknowns).
