The Importance of Project Procurement Management

9 April, 2022

Introduction

In today’s modern world, it’s hard to find a company who doesn’t do some sort of outsourcing. The Merriam-Webster Dictionary defines outsourcing as “to procure (something, such as some goods or services needed by a business or organization) from outside sources and especially from foreign or nonunion suppliers” (Merriam-Webster). There are many reasons why outsourcing is very often the preferred way of companies acquiring the necessary resources. Some of the reasons often include better prices overseas or outside the country, cheaper labor, less legal restrictions, among others. This term leads us to a word very close in meaning: procurement. The aforementioned Dictionary defines procurement as “to obtain (something) by particular care and effort” (Merriam-Webster). Purchasing products and services from a third party is referred to as procurement. In the government, the phrase procurement is generally used; in the private sector, the terms purchase and outsourcing are commonly employed. Suppliers, vendors, contractors, subcontractors, and sellers are names used to describe organizations or persons who offer procurement services; among these categories, suppliers is the most often used.

Importance of Procurement

Many IT initiatives necessitate the usage of items and services obtained from outside the company. By simple linguistics, an outside source is a source that is not part of a given team or company. This means that the project team can be a supplier to another group within the company, or the project team can be a supplier to another group inside the organization. Many IT departments in corporations compete directly with outside suppliers, and they must follow the same criteria definitions, statements of work, and bids. Regardless of who offers the services to whom, the principles and techniques of solid project procurement processes are good to follow.

Advantages of Outsourcing

Now let’s explore the advantages and disadvantages of outsourcing. There are at least four major advantages in play when companies decide to outsource: cost, access to skills, manufacturing capabilities, and taking advantage of time zones (Logue, 2021). Outsourcing an activity is frequently less expensive than hiring employees to perform the task. This is especially true if the task may be outsourced to a region of the country where the average wage is lower or to a country where the pay rate is much lower. One reason a company might outsource is to acquire access to expertise and services it wouldn't otherwise be able to get. A small firm, for example, may not be able to afford to maintain a high-end graphic designer on staff, but it may be able to hire one to design a logo. Many organizations have discovered that outsourcing allows them to locate higher-level programming, bookkeeping, and human resources talents than hiring in-house. Many businesses excel in product design and marketing but lack the capacity to create them. To obtain the items, they hire a business that specializes in manufacturing. Many firms have discovered that outsourcing work to workers in other time zones allows them to do more work in less time. By outsourcing to call centers in India and the Philippines, for example, a US corporation may provide 24-hour customer assistance. When a Swedish investment bank hires freelance editors in the United States to work on reports while Europe is sleeping, it can turn them around faster.

Procurement Phases and Planning

Once a company has decided on which goods to procure from which company, there are four steps in order to secure said goods. The steps, in order, are: plan procurement management, conduct procurements, control procurements, and close procurements (Landau, 2018). Procurements are first identified during the project's planning phase. A statement of work (SOW) is required for every external contractor to serve as a document defining the job to be contracted. Prior to the contract, however, a request for proposal is issued, in which different contractors are invited to bid on the task and the project manager is able to select who will be awarded the contract based on their proposals. These requests are well-thought-out, as they serve as project guiding papers. It's preferable if they're as specific as possible. This prevents subsequent misunderstanding and aids in the development of more precise plans. The procurement management plan, which comprises requirement papers, a risk register, activity resource needs, a project timeline, activity cost estimates, and more, collects this information. There are tools and approaches to assist guide these decisions, such as make-or-buy analysis, which determines if a task requires an external provider or can be completed in-house. Getting professional advice, conducting market research, and meeting with stakeholders may all assist you make this decision.

Conducting Procurements

Following the completion of the first phase's documentation, the conduct procurement step involves analyzing the bids received and deciding which one to accept. However, before making a decision, a criterion should be in place to determine which proposal is better for the project and suits your logistics management. Following that, the contracts are signed, and the project management plan is revised. Determine the winner by conferring with bidders, use methodologies for analyzing offers, and obtaining independent estimates to ensure that the bids are within the typical range. It's never a bad idea to seek the opinion of specialists in the fields where you're contracting. You can also use analytical approaches to help you. Advertising allows you to cast the biggest net possible, allowing you to make decisions based on all potential bids. Then there will be procurement talks to fine-tune the final contracts to fit both your and the contractor's requirements. A purchase order should be used to document the price, quantity, delivery window, and payment conditions for the goods/services you want. It's a legally enforceable contract that ensures you and your vendor are on the same page. Our purchase order template can assist you in creating one that is appropriate for your project.

Procurement Control

Once the contracts are signed, the contractors' management must be integrated into the overall management duties. Contractors may wreak havoc on finances and deadlines, causing a project to go behind schedule or worse. As a result, regular status updates are required to evaluate contractor agreements, get progress reports, and assess job performance to ensure that contractors are following the contract criteria. Even if you engage contractors because they are specialists in their field, you must still supervise and track their work to ensure that it is moving according to plan. To ensure that the work is progressing well, contract a change control system and conduct frequent procurement performance reviews, including inspections and audits. Managers benefit from performance reports as well. A payment system, as well as a claims administration and records management system, must be in place. To assist performance monitoring, work orders must include full details regarding the job being performed by contractors.

Finalizing Procurements

Just as there must be a mechanism in place to begin the procurement, there must also be one in place to complete it. What constitutes finished work should be specified in the original agreement with the contractor so that neither party is confused about when the job is completed. A formal release of liability is frequently required for insurance and bonding. This ensures that there are no pending modifications to the contract's value and completion date. Procurement audits help with this process, as well as having structured procurement negotiations. A records management system will also be needed to manage all the paperwork that will be involved with this stage of the procurement process.
