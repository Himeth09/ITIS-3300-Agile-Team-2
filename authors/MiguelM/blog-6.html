Keys To Managing And Leading People

19 March, 2022

Introduction

One of the most important and at the same time difficult aspects of Project Management is managing and leading people effectively. Where to start? What does it entail? What are the best practices? One of the most influential aspects that affect people’s performance at work is the psychological one. This affects not only performance, but also other factors like motivation, leadership, influence, and the like. Luckily, there are many scientists and researchers who have paved the way in this field and have shed much needed knowledge into the factors that affect a person’s psychological state of being.

Motivation Types

Why do people act the way they do? Is there a reason? Is there a pattern? An expert from the University of Rochester defines extrinsic motivation as “completing a task or exhibiting a behavior because of outside causes such as avoiding punishment or receiving a reward” (Sennett, 2021). Additionally, Sennett also defines intrinsic motivation as “performing a task because it’s personally rewarding to you” (Sennett, 2021). In other words, there are two different directions that shape the reason why someone acts at any given time. In certain circumstances, extrinsic incentive is advantageous. Working for a reward, for example, might be beneficial when you need to finish an activity that you would otherwise dislike. Intrinsic motivation, on the other hand, is usually a more successful long-term strategy for attaining objectives and completing activities in a way that makes you happy. Extrinsic motivation can be beneficial in some settings, but it can also contribute to burnout or loss of efficacy over time. Intrinsic and extrinsic motivation can sometimes complement each other to help you finish a job. If you have a job and are working on a project, for example, you may be extrinsically driven to complete it in order to meet a teammate's deadline. It's possible that you're organically motivated to do it because you enjoy the endeavor and want to perform well.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow's hierarchy of needs is a psychological motivational theory that consists of a five-tier model of human wants, which is sometimes shown as hierarchical tiers within a pyramid (McLeod, 2007). Physiological (food and clothes), safety (job security), love and belonging needs (friendship), esteem, and self-actualization are the needs from the bottom of the hierarchy up. Individuals must attend to lower-level demands before they can attend to higher-level requirements. There are two types of requirements in this five-stage model: deficit and growth. Deficiency needs (D-needs) correspond to the first four levels, whereas growth or being needs refers to the highest level (B-needs). Deficiency needs emerge as a result of deprivation, and they are believed to motivate people when they are not satisfied. Furthermore, the longer such wants go unmet, the stronger the drive to meet them becomes. Physiological needs - these are biological need for human life, such as air, food, drink, shelter, clothes, warmth, sex, and sleep, to name a few. The human body cannot operate efficiently if these demands are not met. Once an individual's physiological demands have been met, the need for security and safety becomes more important. After physiological and safety requirements are met, the third level of human wants is social, which includes emotions of belonging. Self-worth, accomplishment, and respect are among the fourth level of Maslow's hierarchy of needs. The highest level in Maslow's hierarchy, self-actualization needs relate to the realization of a person's potential, self-fulfillment, personal progress, and peak experiences.

Herzberg's Motivation-Hygiene Theory

The two-factor motivation theory, also known as Herzberg's motivation-hygiene theory or dual-factor theory, claims that there are two sets of mutually incompatible elements that generate job satisfaction or unhappiness in the workplace (Nickerson, 2021). In general, these variables that promote job happiness have to do with self-development and self-actualization. Motivators, according to Herzberg, assured job contentment, but a lack of hygienic elements bred job satisfaction. Since then, the two-factor motivation theory has become one of the most often utilized theoretical frameworks in the field of job satisfaction research. Maslow (1954), Herzberg, Vroom (1964), Alderfer (1972), McCalland (1961), and Locke et al. (1981) — the key mid-twentieth-century motivation researchers — produced studies that Basset-Jones and Lloyd suggest may be separated into content and process theories of motivation. Content theories, such as those proposed by Herzberg et al. (1959), imply a complex interplay between internal and external elements and investigate how humans respond to various internal and external stimuli. Meanwhile, process theories, such as Vroom's (1964), look at how internal variables influence a person's behavior.

McClelland's Acquired-Needs Theory

Instead of the two categories outlined in Herzberg's theory, psychologist David McClelland's acquired-needs theory divides employee needs into three. Achievement, affinity, and power are the three categories (lumen). Employees that are highly accomplishment motivated are motivated by a desire to master new skills. They like to work on activities of intermediate complexity where the results are the result of their effort rather than chance. They value constructive criticism on their work. Affiliation-motivated employees are motivated by the need to form and maintain social ties. They want to feel loved and welcomed and appreciate being a part of a group. They may not be successful managers because they are overly concerned with how others see them. Power-motivated employees are motivated by a desire to influence, teach, or inspire others. They like their jobs and place a strong emphasis on discipline. They may, however, take a zero-sum approach to group labor, in which one person must lose or fail for another to win or succeed. However, if directed correctly, this may assist individuals in the group feel capable while also supporting collective goals.

McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y

Douglas McGregor, a social psychologist, established two opposing ideas in the 1960s to explain how managers' perceptions about what drives their employees might influence their management style (MindTools). These he labeled Theory X and Theory Y. Even now, these theories are still relevant. Theory X supervisors have a gloomy attitude toward their employees, assuming that they are innately uninspired and detest their jobs. Work in such companies may be monotonous, and individuals are frequently motivated using a "carrot and stick" strategy. Performance assessments and remuneration are often based on measurable outcomes, like as sales numbers or product output, and are used to monitor and regulate employees. Theory Y managers have a positive attitude toward their employees and manage in a decentralized, participatory manner. This motivates managers and their team members to work together more collaboratively and trustingly. Managers push employees to enhance their abilities and recommend improvements as they take on more responsibility. Appraisals are held on a regular basis, but they are intended to foster open dialogue rather than to manage employees, as they are in Theory X businesses.

Conclusion

In conclusion, project management touches on a variety of critical subjects such as motivation, influence, power, effectiveness, emotional intelligence, and leadership. Project managers and team members must grasp and practice essential ideas linked to these issues since projects are done by and for people. Remember that everyone loves to work with individuals they like and respect, therefore treating others with respect, regardless of their title or position, is essential. This is especially true for persons who work in support professions like administrative assistants, security guards, and cleaning crew members. Everyone has an important part to play.
